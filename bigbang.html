Everything on this site — everything anyone has ever written, built, or felt — sits at the very end of a much longer walk. This is that walk, from the first instant to right now.

the cosmic calendar

If the universe were one year old

Astronomer Carl Sagan popularized this device: compress 13.8 billion years into a single calendar year. On this scale, one day equals roughly 38 million real years — and every human being who has ever lived fits into the final seconds of December 31st.

waypoint 01 — the first instants

What happened in the first 380,000 years

In the tiniest fractions of a second after the Big Bang, the universe went through changes more extreme than anything since.

0 – 10⁻⁴³ seconds

The Planck Epoch

The earliest moment physics can describe. Gravity, electromagnetism, and the nuclear forces are thought to be unified into a single force. Our current theories break down before this point.

~10⁻³² seconds

Cosmic Inflation

The universe undergoes an incomprehensibly fast expansion, growing by a factor of at least 10²⁶ in a fraction of a second, smoothing the universe out and planting the seeds of future galaxies.

First few minutes

Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

As the universe cools past a billion degrees, protons and neutrons fuse into the first light nuclei — mostly hydrogen and helium, with trace lithium.

~380,000 years

Recombination & First Light

Electrons bind to nuclei, forming neutral atoms. Light is freed to travel unobstructed for the first time — the afterglow is the cosmic microwave background, still detectable today.

waypoint 02 — dark ages, first light

For hundreds of millions of years, no stars at all

Only cooling gas drifting in the dark. Then gravity did its slow work.

380,000 yrs – ~150M yrs

The Cosmic Dark Ages

With no stars yet formed, the universe is filled with neutral hydrogen and helium gas and very little light, while gravity quietly pulls denser pockets together.

~100 – 250 million years

The First Stars

Enormous, short-lived stars made purely of hydrogen and helium ignite, forging the first heavier elements and scattering them in supernova explosions.

~400M – 1B years

First Galaxies

Star clusters merge and gas collapses further, building the first small galaxies. Ultraviolet light from young stars re-ionizes the surrounding hydrogen gas.

~8.8 billion years

The Milky Way Forms

Our home galaxy assembles gradually through mergers with smaller galaxies, building up its spiral disk over billions of years.

waypoint 03 — our sun and earth

An ordinary cloud of dust becomes home

About two-thirds of the way through cosmic history, a star ignites — and the planet we call home takes shape around it.

4.6 billion years ago

The Sun Ignites

A cloud of gas and dust, disturbed perhaps by a nearby supernova, collapses under gravity. At its center, fusion ignites — the Sun is born, ringed by a spinning disk of leftover material.

4.54 billion years ago

Earth Forms

Within that disk, dust grains stick together, then rocks, then planet-sized bodies. Earth accretes from countless collisions, starting out as a molten world.

~4.5 billion years ago

The Moon is Born

A Mars-sized body collides with the young Earth. Debris from the impact coalesces into the Moon, which later stabilizes Earth's tilt and drives its tides.

4.1 – 3.8 billion years ago

Heavy Bombardment

Earth and its neighbors are pelted by asteroids and comets left over from planet formation — some of which may have delivered water and organic molecules.

waypoint 04 — life begins

Where chemistry crossed into biology

It remains one of science's deepest open questions — but the fossil and chemical evidence narrows the window considerably.

~3.8 – 3.5 billion years ago

The First Life

The earliest widely accepted evidence of life — simple, single-celled microbes — appears in the rock record. All known life today descends from this common ancestor.

~2.4 billion years ago

The Great Oxidation Event

Photosynthetic microbes begin pumping oxygen into the atmosphere for the first time, poisoning most existing life but opening the door for oxygen-breathing organisms.

~2.1 billion years ago

Complex Cells Emerge

Eukaryotic cells — with a nucleus and internal structures — appear, likely when one microbe engulfed another in a partnership that became permanent.

1.5B – 600M years ago

Multicellular Life

Cells begin cooperating permanently as single organisms. Simple multicellular algae, fungi, and eventually early animals appear.

"Every living thing on Earth shares the same basic genetic code."
waypoint 05 — life diversifies

Evolution starts producing an extraordinary range of forms

On scales of hundreds of millions of years, once multicellular life took hold.

541 million years ago

The Cambrian Explosion

In a geologically brief window, most major animal body plans appear for the first time, along with hard shells, complex eyes, and active predation.

~470 million years ago

Life Moves onto Land

Plants, followed by arthropods and eventually vertebrates, colonize dry land for the first time, reshaping Earth's landscapes and atmosphere.

252 – 66 million years ago

The Age of Dinosaurs

After the largest mass extinction in Earth's history at 252 million years ago, dinosaurs rise to dominate land ecosystems for over 180 million years.

66 million years ago

The Chicxulub Impact

An asteroid roughly 10 km wide strikes what is now Mexico, ending the age of non-avian dinosaurs and clearing the way for mammals to diversify.

waypoint 06 — the rise of humans

Less than 0.2% of Earth's history

In the last sliver of geological time, a single species began to reshape the planet.

~65 million years ago

Mammals Diversify

With the dinosaurs gone, mammals rapidly diversify into new ecological niches, including the early primates.

~6 – 7 million years ago

Human and Chimp Lineages Split

The evolutionary line leading to humans diverges from the line leading to chimpanzees, our closest living relatives.

~2.8 million years ago

The Genus Homo Appears

Early Homo species emerge in Africa, with larger brains and the first evidence of stone tool use.

~300,000 years ago

Homo Sapiens

Anatomically modern humans appear in Africa. For most of this time, humans live as small bands of hunter-gatherers.

waypoint 07 — civilization

The last 12,000 years — a blink, in cosmic terms

Nearly the entirety of human civilization as we know it fits inside this window.

~12,000 years ago

The Agricultural Revolution

Independently in several regions, humans begin domesticating plants and animals, enabling permanent settlements.

~5,000 years ago

Writing and Cities

In Mesopotamia, Egypt, and later the Indus Valley and China, writing systems emerge alongside the first cities, laws, and organized states.

~2,500 years ago

Classical Civilizations

Philosophy, mathematics, and formal governance flourish across Greece, Persia, India, and China, alongside empires and trade networks spanning continents.

1760 – 1840

The Industrial Revolution

Steam power and mechanization transform production, travel, and daily life more rapidly than any previous change in human history.

waypoint 08 — now

The most recent chapter is also the most crowded

And it is still being written.

1900s – 1940s

Modern Science

Relativity and quantum mechanics rewrite our understanding of space, time, and matter, while two world wars reshape the global order.

1957 – 1969

The Space Age

Sputnik reaches orbit in 1957; humans walk on the Moon in 1969 — the first steps by life from Earth onto another world.

1970s – 2000s

The Digital Revolution

Computers move from research labs to homes and pockets. The internet connects billions of people into a single, continuously updating web of information.

2026

You Are Here

13.8 billion years of expansion, fusion, chemistry, and evolution have led to this exact moment — someone reading a page, thinking about where they fit in the story.

note Every atom in your body was forged in a star. The calcium in your bones, the iron in your blood, the oxygen you just breathed — all of it was made inside stars that lived and died long before the Sun existed.
