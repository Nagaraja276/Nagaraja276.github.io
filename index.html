waypoint 01 — the road so far

I build my own worldview, one root question at a time.

I didn't inherit the way I think from a teacher, a book, or a community. I built it — through lived experience, through sitting with hard questions instead of settling for the first answer. I'm an atheist because I reasoned my way there, not because of any conflict with how I was raised.

I'm an only child. My father is physically handicapped and has been the greatest influence on how I think about effort and dignity — a man who, in my words, earns people, not money. That's shaped how seriously I take responsibility, and how early I started thinking about the shape of my own future.

"My life is my book — I'm the author and the character, both."

Systems, not symptoms

I look for the structure under a problem — incentives, feedback loops, root causes — rather than reacting to whatever happened most recently.

Slow, on purpose

I don't rush complex questions. Sitting with an idea longer isn't indecision — it's giving the idea the seriousness it's owed.

Root-question orientation

My instinct is "why is this actually happening," not "how do I patch this." It's why cosmology and psychology pull at me as hard as anything practical does.

Documented, not dramatized

When something gets hard, I tend to turn it into a structured record I can act on calmly, rather than carry it around as noise.

waypoint 03 — field notes

When the systems around me break, I take notes.

Alongside the personal writing, I've started documenting the systems I run into — where they fail an ordinary person, and what a better-designed version would look like. This is the practical edge of the same root-question instinct: not just asking why, but proposing what should replace it.
