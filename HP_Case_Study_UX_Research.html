When "Fixed" Isn't Fixed

A first-person research case study on a broken repair-escalation system, documented in real time over a 48-day consumer dispute.

Context

In May 2026, I submitted a laptop (HP 250R G10, purchased for ₹58,100) for warranty repair after a Bluetooth fault that had existed since purchase. What should have been a routine repair became a 48-day dispute involving four motherboard replacements, two additional defects introduced during "repair," a formal legal notice, and an active consumer court filing in preparation.

This case study isn't about whether HP is a good or bad company. It's about something more useful: what happens to an ordinary user when a large organization's support system has no single owner, no reliable source of truth, and no accountability for its own claims. I lived inside that system for seven weeks and documented every interaction as it happened. What follows is what that documentation reveals.

The Core UX Failure

Strip away the specifics, and the system had one structural defect: no one inside it was accountable for the whole problem — only for their own narrow piece of it. This showed up in four repeating patterns.

  1. Fragmented ownership. Across the case, I dealt with rotating contacts — several names on HP's service-head channel, a separate social media contact, the National Consumer Helpline, and the local service centre — none of whom appeared to share a consistent record of what the others had said or done. Two separate HP support accounts once contradicted each other about a repair claim on my own device, minutes apart.
  2. Claims without verification. A claim that my SSD had been replaced originated from an internal miscommunication — a diagnostic swap, done to isolate a fault, got summarized somewhere along HP's chain as an actual replacement, and was passed to me as fact. I only closed this by independently checking Device Manager and Disk Management myself, and getting a written clarification from the technician. The system produced a false claim about my own property, and the burden of disproving it fell entirely on me.
  3. "Fixed" was declared, not verified. The laptop was handed back in person with a verbal assurance it was "ready and working fine." Within hours, it had a total charging failure. The next day, testing revealed the fingerprint sensor — the original defect the case was opened for — was non-functional again, on the fourth motherboard. Nothing in the process required functional verification before a unit was called resolved and handed back.
  4. The user became the system's memory. By the time I'd documented this, I was tracking two postal articles, three separate legal deadlines, four motherboard histories, and a dozen contact threads — because no single part of the process was doing that tracking for me.

The Decision Points

A few moments in this case required a judgment call under incomplete information — the actual skill this case exists to demonstrate, beyond just documentation.

When a claim appeared with no prior record anywhere in the case history, I chose to push back and verify independently rather than accept it or dismiss it outright — asking for the service record while checking the system myself. That combination closed the issue cleanly, with evidence, instead of leaving it as an unresolved dispute.

When a new fault appeared hours after handover, I chose not to intervene myself, and instead documented every troubleshooting step already taken before flagging it — protecting both the warranty and the evidentiary record that the fault was pre-existing.

When two legal deadlines were running in parallel, I chose to track them independently rather than assume they were the same clock. They weren't — conflating them would have cost a real legal option.

What a Well-Designed Resolution System Would Look Like

If I were designing the process that should have existed:

  • A single named case owner, not a rotating cast of contacts, with authority to see the entire history — not just their department's slice of it.
  • A verified, timestamped source of truth for every claim that both the company and the customer can see — not claims relayed and later walked back.
  • A mandatory functional checklist before any unit is marked "resolved" — every subsystem that was ever faulty gets tested and signed off before handover.
  • A default assumption that the customer's independent verification is valid evidence, not something to be second-guessed.
  • Automatic compounding-defect detection — a system that flags a recurring fault instead of treating each occurrence as a fresh, unrelated ticket.

None of this requires new technology. It requires treating case continuity and verification as the product, not an afterthought to the repair itself.

The Broader Pattern

This case is one laptop, one company, one person. But the failure mode isn't unique to any single company — it's the default shape of most large-scale consumer support systems, because each department optimizes for closing its own ticket, not for the customer's continuous experience across the whole process.

"The gap between what a company says its process is, and what a user actually experiences, is where real harm happens — and someone has to be able to see and name that gap clearly enough to fix it."

What made this case survivable, rather than a total loss of time and money, wasn't luck — it was building, by hand, the exact structure the system should have provided: a single continuous record, independent verification at each step, and tracked deadlines.
